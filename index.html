About Me

Welcome to my corner of the web! I am currently a PhD candidate at the Max Planck Institute for Astromomy in Heidelberg where I'm looking to finish my doctorate degree in 2028. As a part of the protoplanets group in the planet and star formation department, I'm supervised by Myriam Benisty. My research focuses on improving our understanding of planet formation through constraining the conditions in the inner regions of protoplanetary discs observationally.

We have detected thousands of exoplanets with a huge diversity in terms of intrinsic characteristics (mass, size, composition, atmosphere, ...) and orbital radii. At the same time, for instance using the powerful (sub-)mm interferometer ALMA, we can detect and spatially resolve hundreds of protoplanetary discs, which we think are the birth places of these planets. While the observed discs also exhibit a wide variety of characteristics, including substructures like gaps, rings, and spirals, which could be caused by embedded planents, the exact connection between planetary systems and their progentior discs is yet to be found. In particular, most exoplanets are detected close to their host star, well within 10au. Observationally, however, we've mostly focused on the outer, colder disc outside of 10au. My research focuses instead on these inner disc regions, looking at emission at shorter wavelengths in the infrared, which trace gas at a few hundreds to thousands of Kelvin. To achieve this, we can use ground based instruments such as the Very Large Telescope (VLT), or the upcoming Extremely Large Telescope (ELT), as well as space based instruments like JWST.

When I'm not peering through telescopes or analyzing data, I'm committed to making astronomy accessible to everyone through outreach programs and public talks.

Research Projects

🌌

Spectrally Resolved Observations of the Inner Regions of Transition Discs

Using the high-resolution spectrograph CRIRES+ mounted at the VLT, we observe CO emission at 4.7 microns originating from warm CO gas in the inner disc regions. With CRIRES+, we can resolve the emission lines spectrally, understand the Keplerian broadening contributing to the line shape, and thus pin-point the emitting region radially. Simultaneously, we can perform spectro-astrometry and monitor the displacement of the photocentre along the CRIRES+ slit, which traces the physical extent of the emitting region. Combined, this allows us to derive the exact origin of the CO emission, as well as the local emitting conditions (temperature, density), and deviations from a Keplerian disc (for instance due to a wind, or warp).

Investigation of CO Overtone Emission around Herbig Stars

Very few intermediate mass YSO's (Herbig Stars) exhibit the ro-vibrational overtone emission of the CO molecule. This emission requires CO at particularly high temperatures and column densities - conditions not easily met in protoplanetary discs. In fact, in the targetted sample of Herbig stars, the CO overtone emission only originates from a very close-in region within the dust sublimation front. Here, we combine high-resolution spectroscopy using CRIRES+ and high spatial resolution observations using GRAVITY to unambiguously constrain the CO overtone origin and physical conditions in the inner dust-free disc around Herbig Stars.
